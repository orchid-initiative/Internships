How it works

Three moves. One summer.

01

Hosts post a brief

A structured intake form turns your idea into a defined project — with a real goal, a real "done," and a real time budget on both sides.

02

Interns apply

Students fill out a calibration profile — experience, tools, learning goals, availability timing — so we can match well instead of matching fast.

03

Orchid seeds a workspace

Matched pairs can work within a pre-configured internship repo with a project-tuned AI guidance file, a journal vault, and communication pipelines — ready before the first day.

What we build around

Four pillars.

Scoped problems

Every project opens with a locked Problem Brief: goal, non-goals, acceptance criteria, and a realistic time budget. Scope amendments require an explicit sponsor note — drift is the most common failure mode, and the brief is what catches it early.

Honest communication

Weekly sponsor digests, written agendas for 1:1s, decision records for non-reversible choices, and a stuck protocol that forces clarity before escalation. Interns learn that how they document and present their work matters as much as what they build.

Disciplined tooling

Claude Code on a working Mac/Linux environment, a pre-configured repo with git safeguards, conventional commits with journal-queryable trailers, and an Obsidian vault for private reflection and shared artifacts.

Protected host time

Sponsors stay bounded at 30 minutes a week — digest, async reviews, and structured touchpoints. A mentor — typically from your team, sometimes matched by Orchid — works alongside the intern 1–3 hours a week. Orchid scaffolds the rest.
