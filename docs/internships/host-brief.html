1 · About you

Just enough to follow up. The person filling this out is the project's Sponsor — see Section 5 for the Mentor role. We don't share contact info publicly without asking.

e.g., Executive Director, PI, Founder, Engineering Lead.
What kind of host are you?*
Have you hosted an intern before? Helps us calibrate how much hand-holding to offer on the program-rhythm side.
2 · Project at a glance

A clear brief is the strongest predictor of a strong summer. Don't worry about polish — we'll sharpen it on the call.

Short and concrete. "Replace our donor CSV pipeline with a real database" beats "Data improvements."
Finish this sentence: "An intern who joins this project will…"
What's the problem, why it matters, who benefits, and what would shipped look like? 150–400 words is plenty. 0 words
2–3 observable things that would tell you the work is done. "Donors can be filtered by zip in under 200ms" beats "the system is faster."
What is explicitly out of scope this summer? This protects everyone — the most common cause of a rough summer is silent scope creep.

5 · Time, roles & duration

We design around two host-side roles — a Sponsor (project owner) and a Mentor (day-to-day technical contact) — plus the time each can commit. For most projects the mentor is naturally someone on your team or in your lab; for smaller, one-off projects, Orchid can match an outside mentor.

Sponsor time per week* The project owner's time — reading the digest, async PR reviews, structured touchpoints.
Who will the day-to-day Mentor be?* The person who works closely with the intern — weekly 1:1, ad-hoc questions, routine PR reviews. Roughly 1–3 hours a week.
Mentor time available per week
Domain expertise, mentoring style, time-zone overlap, anything else.
Sponsor response time for escalated questions* Day-to-day questions go to the mentor. This is for the things that need the project owner.
Program length*
Preferred start window*
Grant report, conference, product launch, board meeting — anything that fixes when "done" has to be done.
